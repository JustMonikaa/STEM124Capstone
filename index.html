Weeks 1-3

Phase 1: Market Validation & Patent Search

Stop asking "What do I want to build?" and start asking "What problem actually exists?" We avoid the common trap of re-inventing the wheel by using Prior Art Search before we write a single word of the manuscript.

1

The Gap Analysis

Do not use Scribd. Go to Google Patents and IPOPHIL.

Weeks 4-7

Phase 2: The Agile Build Sprints

We ditch the "Waterfall" method. You will not build one perfect thing at the end. You will build Fast, Fail Early, and Iterate. This is how real engineering works.

Weeks 4-5

Sprint 1: The "Ugly" Prototype (MVP)

Goal: Functionality ONLY. Use cardboard, duct tape, and breadboards. No soldering yet. Does the sensor read data? Does the code compile?

Deliverable: Working Circuit / Skeleton
Weeks 6-7

Sprint 2: Integration & Enclosure

Goal: Durability. Solder the connections on a PCB (Universal board). 3D print or fabricate the housing. Ensure wires aren't loose.

Deliverable: Field-Ready Device

The Engineering Logbook Protocol

You must maintain a physical notebook. No logbook, no defense. Each entry must be dated and signed. Paste raw receipts, sketch diagrams, and write down every failed attempt. (e.g., "Oct 12: Sensor burned out. Reason: connected 5V to 3.3V pin.")

Weeks 8-10

Phase 3: Reliability & Testing

In Practical Research 2, you learned to test a hypothesis. In Capstone, you test a Product. We focus on Mean Time Between Failures (MTBF) and User Acceptance Testing (UAT).

1. Efficiency Testing

Does it actually work? Compare your device against the manual method or a commercial product.

Trial 1 Manual: 45m Device: 12m
Trial 2 Manual: 42m Device: 11m
Trial 3 Manual: 48m Device: 13m

2. User Acceptance (UAT)

Let the target users (from Phase 1) touch it. Do not explain how to use it. Watch them struggle. This is data.

The "Mom Test"

If your mom can't figure out how to turn it on in 30 seconds, your UX failed. Iterate.

Weeks 11-13

Phase 4: The Defense

The panel isn't looking for a Nobel prize. They are looking for: 1) Did you build it yourself? 2) Does it work? 3) Do you understand the science?

Weeks Complete

The Grading Rubric Survival Guide

Based on standard Engineering Capstone Rubrics (Source: ISEF/DepEd Guidelines)

A

Engineering Goals (40%)

Prototype is fully functional. "Dead" prototypes (those that broke 'just this morning') receive automatic deductions. Wiring is neat and enclosed.

B

Scientific Thought (30%)

Data analysis uses correct stats (see Tools). Variables were controlled during testing. Claims are backed by your specific data, not general knowledge.

C

Process & Agility (20%)

Evidence of iteration. Show the "V1" that failed. Show the logbook. Prove you solved problems rather than just copying a tutorial.

D

Presentation (10%)

10 minutes max. Slides are visual (photos/graphs), not walls of text. Q&A is answered confidently by all members, not just the leader.
